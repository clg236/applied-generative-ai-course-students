## What this course is about

This is an applied course about using generative AI in business. We will examine what the models can do, where they fail, how they fit into a larger system, and what evidence supports a business decision.

Students will build, test, and revise model-backed work. The emphasis is not on one vendor or tool. It is on defining a useful task, selecting an appropriate system, evaluating the result, managing risk and cost, and explaining what should remain under human control.

Con Edison is the course partner. The team capstone asks how generative AI and related analytical systems might support a real infrastructure decision in New York City. Current problem areas include manhole and infrastructure image classification, AMI communication triage, rooftop-solar records reconciliation, and making underground-network information easier for authorized people to use. Con Edison may refine these areas or add briefs during the course.

## Learning goals

By the end of the course, you should be able to:

1. Distinguish AI, machine learning, deep learning, generative AI, chatbots, and agents in plain language.
2. Explain how model training, tokens, context, tools, and system instructions affect an output.
3. Turn a business need into a bounded task with a clear user, input, output, decision, and review boundary.
4. Build a useful generative-AI asset, tool, workflow, or prototype without needing to write all of the code yourself.
5. Evaluate quality, failure, cost, latency, privacy, and risk using evidence from the actual task.
6. Make and defend a practical recommendation, including what remains uncertain and what a person must review.

## Assignments

The syllabus defines each assignment's purpose, weight, and major deadline. The current assignment page in Icerynk is the source of truth for its detailed requirements, examples, submission instructions, and approved alternatives.

### Six build logs

After each session, submit a short record of what you tried, what surprised you or failed, what you will change, and how you used AI. These are graded for specific and honest reflection, not writing polish.

### Project 1 · Individual model comparison

Define one bounded business task, test at least two model-backed versions under comparable conditions, revise from the evidence, and make a recommendation. The [Project 1 brief](../assignments/project-1/brief.md) contains the current evaluation and submission requirements.

### Project 2 · Individual applied build

Create a useful asset, tool, workflow, or another approved equivalent. Demonstrate the result and document the important decisions, failures, changes, and switching costs. The Project 2 page in the relevant Icerynk session contains the current requirements when it is released.

### Capstone · Team project

In a team of three or four, address a Con Edison partner brief or another approved New York infrastructure problem. Deliver a working or reviewable project, a concise written brief, and a twelve-minute presentation with evidence-based Q&A. The capstone and partner-brief pages in the relevant Icerynk sessions contain the current requirements and working problem areas when they are released.

You may propose a different format when it demonstrates the same learning goals. Get approval before the deadline. Tools, examples, and intermediate formats may be refined as the course develops; changes will be announced in writing and will not increase the overall workload, move a deadline earlier, or disadvantage work already completed.

Across assignments, strong work:

- produces a working or reviewable result;
- uses appropriate evidence and tests, including failures;
- explains limits, risks, cost, and human-review needs; and
- clearly discloses the student's own decisions and AI use.

The assignment page provides the task-specific review criteria. Grades and private feedback are recorded in Brightspace. ## AI use, collaboration, and academic integrity

AI use is expected unless an assignment says otherwise. You remain responsible for the accuracy, sources, permissions, decisions, and final work. With each submission, briefly identify the tools or models used, what they contributed, and where your judgment changed or rejected the output.

Project 1 and Project 2 are individual assignments. Class activities may use pairs or groups, and the capstone is team work. Credit every source and collaborator. Do not fabricate evidence, present another person's work as your own, or use AI disclosure as a substitute for attribution.

Follow the [NYU Academic Integrity Policy](https://www.nyu.edu/about/policies-guidelines-compliance/policies-and-guidelines/academic-integrity-for-students-at-nyu.html) and the [NYU Stern Code of Conduct](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/portal-partners/student-engagement/student-life/life-stern/code-conduct).
